The Essential 38 Portland Restaurants, October '14

Updated by Danielle Centoni on Oct 8, 2014, 1:54p

  • Share
  • Tweet
  • Share

It's time to update the Eater 38, your answer and ours to any question that begins, "Can you recommend a restaurant...?" This highly elite group covers the entire city, spans myriad cuisines, and collectively satisfies all of your restaurant needs, save for those occasions when you absolutely must spend half a paycheck. Every couple of months, we'll be adding pertinent restaurants that were omitted, have newly become eligible (restaurants must be open at least six months), or have stepped up their game.

This time around, after much reflection, we must make room by retiring three stalwarts: Taqueria Y Panaderia Santa Cruz , Nuestra Cocina, and Levant. In their place we welcome Davenport, a neighborhood joint with a refined touch, Kachka, the cozy house of Russian delights, and Taqueria Nueve, Portland's prodigal son.

Click the icon above to see our 38 picks in list order. Don't see your favorite? Think we're crazy? Lobby for or against in the comments below.

1 Apizza Scholls

In a years-long, very close race with Ken's Artisan Pizza, Apizza Scholls stills emerges as the town's best pie. Co-owner/pizzaiolo Brian Spangler shows respect for his dough, refusing to overload his crisp, quick-fired crusts with more toppings than they can handle. Thankfully, there's no need for DIY concoctions given the pizzeria's perfected equation of sauce, fresh mozzarella, pecorino romano, and in the case of the Pizza Amore, salty capicola scattered on top. Incidentally, that's where Scholls still stands, despite the recent influx of solid Portland pizza.

4741 SE Hawthorne Blvd

2 ATAULA

Eater's 2013 Chef of the Year Jose Chesa emphasizes a convivial, familial vibe at his restaurant Ataula, which considers itself a Spanish gastropub more than a stuffy "tapas restaurant." The tapas themselves combine similar levels of comforting familiarity and playfulness: beef and potato "bombas" arrive in a suitably spicy sauce; chorizo "lollipops" are addictive, and an almost-savory sangria is the perfect drink for rainy evenings. A Sunday-only Spanish brunch is a don't-miss.

1818 NW 23rd Pl
Portland, OR 97210

(503) 894-8904

3 Ava Gene's

No opening in recent memory has created a fervor quite like Ava Gene's, the polished Italian restaurant by Stumptown's Duane Sorenson and NYC transplant Joshua McFadden (just ask Bon Appetit's Andrew Knowlton, who named the spot the #5 restaurant in the country in 2013). The room itself provides Division's must-visit see-and-be-seen spot, but McFadden's dishes especially toothsome pastas and expertly executed vegetables prove this book should be judged beyond its cover.

3377 SE Division St
Portland, OR 97202

4 Aviary

Who says that too many cooks spoil the broth? At Aviary, three NYC transplants Sarah Pliner, Jasper Shen, and Kat Whitehead present an eclectic menu that actually delivers, with unique plates that reflect the trio's variety of influences; a hint of French here, Asian flair there. Heartier dishes get higher marks: Go for the goat curry, four-cup chicken, and what's easily the spot's signature dish crispy pig ear served over coconut rice.

1733 NE Alberta St
Portland, OR 97211

5 Bamboo Sushi NW

Portland's "green" ethos is no more obvious than at Bamboo the world's first certified-sustainable sushi restaurant where the always-fresh catch plays nicely with unexpected flavor combinations (scallops, meet juniper olive oil and red miso dust). Momentum has shifted from the SE flagship to the NW 23rd location, but we're also keeping an eye on Bamboo's just-opened izakaya concept NE Alberta, and its forthcoming sister izakaya arriving Downtown in early 2015.

310 SE 28th Ave

6 Beast

Naomi Pomeroy, the 2014 James Beard Best Chef NW recipient, rocks the kitchen at Beast where each intimate, prix-fixe seating is like a dinner party gone carnivorously crazy. Charcuterie plates explode with terrine, gelee, tartare, mousse; braised duck, pork shoulder, and beef cheeks make common appearances on the rotating menu.

7 Biwa

One part ramen den, one part izakaya, two parts modernista basement bunker, this late-night chefs' hangout is run by the most improbable Japanese chef in town: a white guy from Michigan. But don't be distracted by Gabe Rosens provenance: This man can noodle and kimchi with the best of them. And the Biwa burger only available during late night has become legendary.

8 Bollywood Theater SE

Chez Panisse alum Troy MacLarty has a blockbuster on his hands with Bollywood Theater, his take on Indian street food. Both the original NE Alberta location and the new outpost on Division Street's "restaurant row" are fun, high-energy spots, but Division gets our vote for its plentiful outdoor seating and cool, curated Indian market. The menus are among Portland's most unique, focusing on less-discovered textures and spices like fried vada pavs, refreshing yogurt-topped papri chaat, and spicy Goan-style shrimp. The flavor-packed Kati is roll so popular McLarty has said he makes an average of 50,000 per year.

3010 SE Division St

9 Brder Nord

Long before queueing up for brunch became a Portlandia punch line (and before dainty Scandinavian food took the culinary world by storm), the original Swedish cafe on SE Clinton drew lines for its wooden boards and mini cast-iron skillets piled high with aebleskivers, smoked trout hash, and potato pancakes. But Broder's new location in North Portland boasts a more ambitious dinner menu, more seating, and a destination-worthy option in what would otherwise be a dining desert.

10 Castagna

By all accounts, chef Justin Woodward is on fire. The inventive chef picks up where culinary wunderkind Matt Lightner left off, deftly continuing Castagna's reputation as the pioneer of the local "au natural" culinary movement: Greens and flowers are foraged by the chef himself, then presented in a molecular style that reveals an authentic expression of the chef/artist at work. Order up the tasting menu, sit back, and try not to whip out your camera phone as one beautiful plate after another arrives.

1752 SE Hawthorne Blvd
Portland, OR 97202

11 Country Cat Dinnerhouse & Bar

The Country Cat is all about American food, prepared by a chef, Adam Sappington, who understands and executes the nuances of old world cuisine. You might be ordering chicken and dumplings, but what you're getting is chicken and dumplings in a sauce so complicated and complex you might think you're in Paris. The fried chicken here is out of control, and Sappington's the guy who made in-house butchery cool.

12 Davenport

Kevin Gibson developed his loyal following over years of market-fresh, creatively conceived, Mediterranean-inspired small plates at Evoe. Then, last fall, he struck out on his own with East Burnsides Davenport (aka Evoe 2.0). The staff is just as relaxed and friendly, but the space is just a touch more formal. And the same dedication to simplicity and freshness reigns, but theres even more refinement, balance and complexity. We never thought we'd say this, but we're actually happy he made the move.

2215 E Burnside St
Portland, OR 97214

(503) 236-8747

13 Expatriate

Beast chef Naomi Pomeroy goes into unexpected territory with this bar across the street from her famed restaurant. Expatriate, a collaboration between Pomeroy and onetime St. Jack bar manager Kyle Webster, pairs expertly mixed cocktails with an Asian-inflected take on drinking food. Drinks are simultaneously complex yet oddly familiar, and this may be the only place in the world where crab rangoon can sit alongside James Beard's onion-and-butter sandwich. Both are delicious, and the result is as escapist as the bar's name suggests.

5424 NE 30th Ave

14 Firehouse Restaurant

NE's beloved (and arguably under-the-radar) neighborhood spot Firehouse offers warmth, literally and figuratively, with its wood-fired oven, inviting room, and plates of Italian comfort (meatballs with tomato and kale, a rotisserie chicken with olive oil mashed potatoes). Chef Matthew Busetto has stepped up his game of late, embracing pairing dinners and special events that emphasize his brand of accessibility and simplicity.

15 Ha VL

Tucked into a SE 82nd strip mall, HA & VL dishes out Vietnamese soup that transcends the more-familiar pho, and you'll eat what they tell you: Just a couple varieties of savor-each-last-drop broth are available each day, but whether it's the crab flake or shrimp cake or ground pork, these noodle soups will have you slurping up every. last. bite. (If you head over after 11a.m., call beforehand to ensure the soup du jour is still available.)

16 Kachka

When Bonnie and Israel Morales opened this charmer last spring, the young couple kicked off a Russian food revolution we never knew we wanted. Kachka is all about snacky Russian drinking food called zakuski. The silky, pork-filled pelmeni dumplings, the briny sprats on butter-slathered pumperknickel, and the herring under a fur coat, a creamy, Russian-style 7-layer dip of diced, pickled deliciousness, are all swoon-worthy -- and even better when washed down with house-infused horseradish vodka.

17 Lardo East

Rick Gencarelli's Lardo joins an impressive local tradition of quality sandwich-making. In the vein of the Bunks and Meat Cheese Breads before, the spot elevates what can be accomplished between two slices of bread, resulting in dripping, flavor-packed pork meatball banh mis and one of Portland's top burgers (and arguably the city's most satisfying fries). These days, Gencarelli has a bona fide empire on this hands with three locations around Portland all made possible by an accessible menu, great tap list, and the chef's commitment to "bringing fat back." The original Southeast location is still our favorite, thanks to the giant patio.

1212 SE Hawthorne Blvd
Portland, OR 97214

18 Laurelhurst Market

At the forefront of Portland's now-famously carnivorous culinary scene, Laurelhurst Market's combination butchery counter, steakhouse, and sandwich stop ushered in a city-wide movement toward meat without the stuffiness seen in steakhouses of yore. The open room (and LM's ambitious bar program) create an energetic atmosphere where diners indulge with a few dozen oysters before their prime cuts; or simply grab a sandwich to-go. And with chef Ben Bettinger now at the helm, LM is set to make an even bigger impact.

3155 E Burnside St

19 Le Pigeon

James Beard Rising Chef winner Gabe Rucker rocks his East Burnside hole-in-the-wall with an open kitchen, an always changing menu, and a firm handle on how to make inventive but mostly accessible food. This is where you'll find unexpected treats like fennel in your tater tots and foie gras in your profiterole, balanced by impossibly comforting beef cheek bourguignon and twists on classic seared foie gras. If you're looking for a "special occasion" meal, invest in the chef's tasting menu.

20 Little Bird Bistro

The second restaurant owned by Le Pigeon chef/Beard Award-winner Gabriel Rucker, Little Bird provides the more classic (yet surprisingly casual) counterpart to Rucker's now-famously bold take on French cooking. At this power-lunch spot, chef Erik Van Kley serves expertly prepared takes on Parisian bistro favorites like crispy veal sweetbreads, chicken liver mousse, and cassoulet.

21 MURICE

With the opening of her insanely charming restaurant/cafe Maurice, Kristen D. Murray delivered exactly what Portland didn't know it needed: a "pastry luncheonette" that focused equal attention on sweet, savory, and exquisite place settings. The result is one of the city's top leisurely lunch/dessert experiences (it earned a #9 spot on Bon Appetit's 2014 Best New Restaurant list), complete with artfully plated black pepper cheesecakes, warm, soft Meyer lemon pudding cakes, and brioche tartes with sauted sweetbreads.

22 Ned Ludd

The gimmick at this Northeast restaurant is that chef Jason French cooks everything (seriously everything) in a wood-fired oven. But theres absolutely nothing gimmicky about what emerges from the fire: whole roasted trout, ash-kissed seasonal vegetables, bright salads, and stunning culinary experiences like "pork noodles" made entirely from pork fat. Ned Ludd also happens to be Portland best-kept brunch secret, and last spring they launched raucous "pizza night" Mondays.

23 Nongs Khao Man Gai

Nong's Khao Man Gai finally makes a play at world domination with the debut of a proper sit-down restaurant space. Here (and at Nong's two food cart locations, at SW Alder and the PSU cart pod) Nong's signature dish, a deceptively simple take on Hainan chicken, takes center stage. Ask five acolytes their favorite part of the dish and each will offer a different answer: the soul-satisfying broth, the rice, Nong's garlic-heavy sauce, or the chicken itself, skins or no.

24 Nostrana

Featuring intensely regional food, wood-fired oven pizza, and a totally accessible menu, Cathy Whims' Nostrana is a consistent, comforting presence in the local dining scene. The menu embraces a "neighborhood" vibe Meatball Mondays! Gnocchi Thursdays! and the space's barrel-vaulted, exposed-wood ceiling, and tables of chatting guests add to the casual atmosphere. Nostrana straddles that line between "casual-ish" and upscale, and Whims has years of James Beard nominations to prove it.

25 Olympic Provisions SE

It's best known for its meaty goodness Olympic Provisions famously boasts Oregons first USDA certified meat-curing facility but venture past the charcuterie plate into an omnivore's paradise. We love the NW location, but the original SE spot still holds claim to our heart thanks to chef Alex Yoder's rustic takes on shaved Brussels sprouts, marinated anchovies, and roasted cod. The weekend brunch is one of the city's most underrated.

26 Ox

Husband-and-wife team Greg and Gabrielle Quionez Denton have lit up Portland's dining scene with their Argentine-inspired restaurant Ox, a spot where wood-grilled simplicity meets evil-genius concoctions like sweetbread "croutons" and house-made blood sausage. Though meat hitting the grill is the main focus (skirt steak and lamb chops are tops), both veggies and seafood represent well in homey dishes like hominy stew and braised octopus. It's best to get in line early, though dinner wait times can be nursed at the neighboring Whey Bar (now with raw bar).

2225 NE Martin Luther King Jr Blvd

27 Paley's Place

This is the Balthazar of Portland, the place that manages to be creative, yet so classic. Whether youre enjoying mussels frites, escargots, or really interesting northwest takes on classic French-style cooking, head chef Vitaly Paley remains playful and inventive while never veering too far off the path. For those without the luxury of expense-account dining, a newish happy hour menu makes the restaurant more accessible to those looking for a $5 Old Fashioned or $2 oysters.

28 Podnah's Pit BBQ

Podnahs hits the lowest common denominator of what makes food satisfying: theres salt, sweetness, fat, and Rodney Muirheads sauce has the perfect acidity. The brisket is perfectly smoked, the meats some of the best in the city (fanatics fight to the death for the smoked prime rib specials), but what makes Podnahs the greatest is its total lack of pretension. Breakfast service is one of the city's best under-the-radar options.

29 Pok Pok

Classically trained chef Andy Ricker moves to Thailand, learns to cook Thai food better than the locals, and serves up enough Vietnamese chicken wings, boar collar, and noodle soups to snag himself a James Beard Award for Best Chef Northwest. Ricker's now a certified empire-builder, but it's worth a trip to the mothership if you want to never look at Thai food the same way again.

30 Roe

It's a bit of a splurge, but chef Trent Pierce offers an unrivaled approach to seafood at the back-room, reservation-only Roe, where his multi-course tasting menu offers inspired takes on fresh fish and foraged greens. Hidden in the rear of Pierce's more casual seafood restaurant Block + Tackle, Roe offers a conceptual opposite, highlighting modernist techniques in dishes that often hit unexpected notes. Two tasting menus are available each night: a $70 four-course "guest's choice," and a "$95 seven-course chef's tasting.

31 Smallwares

Chef Johanna Ware brings a New York sensibility to her NE spot Smallwares, serving unapologetically "inauthentic" Asian plates with a late-night, rock 'n' roll attitude. Ware's dishes look elegantly composed grilled octopus sits atop a schmear of miso pumpkin puree; short ribs are layered on corn cakes, Napoleon-style but pack a major punch, hitting all the savory, salty, and spicy notes (with "surprise" thrown in for good measure).

32 Sweedeedee

This neighborhood brunch spot is almost painfully Portland: It boasts shelves of Pinterest-ready mason jars, cassette tapes, and mismatched vintage table settings. It serves only breakfast and brunch. And of course, there's that name, which... yeah. But even the most cynical of hearts can't help but submit to its charms. In a land of bacon-heavy brunches, Sweedeedee's breakfast plates arrive loaded with baked eggs and trout, avocados and greens, and other simple delights. And everything you've heard about its salted honey pie is true. Don't skip it.

5202 N Albina Ave
Portland, OR 97217

33 Restaurant St Jack

St. Jack, Eater's 2011 Restaurant of the Year, creates the ultimate bouchon experience in this NW bistro. Embrace "Non, je ne regrette rein" as your official dining motto and sit back and relax with a few whiskey cocktails, plates of rich escargot gratin and roasted bone marrow. When specials are available, snatch them up, particularly the pied de cochon and stuffed duck neck.

34 Tanuki

Yes, it can be difficult to navigate, but follow the cardinal rule don't be an asshole and the charms of Janis Martin's underground drinking den fully emerge. (Note: You must find Japanese zombie/porn flicks, stupidly affordable happy hour, and lots and lots of sake "charming" for this deal to apply.) Invest in the chef's omakase and watch as piles of plates arrive to the table: spiced duck hearts, oysters with kimchi shaved ice, hamachi collar, and whatever else Martin may have up her sleeve.

35 Taqueria Nueve (T9)

Taqueria Nueve It was one of Portlands most beloved taco joints -- authentic, but not slavish, experimental, but not foolish. Then chef/owner Billy Shumaker expanded with a slightly upscale Pearl District spot called D.F., and lost them both in the gamble. Five years later, the original is back in action at the former Beaker & Flask space, offering some of the tastiest, most affordable margaritas and tacos (the carnitas and carne asada are standouts), plus a ceviche-topped caesar salad that will haunt your dreams.

727 SE Washington St
Portland, OR 97214

36 Tasty 'N Sons

John Gorham's insanely popular second spot reinvents Portland's favorite meal brunch taking it to new heights worth the inevitable wait. The sprawling, family-style menu flaunts influences from North Africa to Asia to the American South, with a strong "put an egg on it ethos." Think shakshuka with merguez sausage, steak and eggs with corncakes, and a soul-soothingly simple polenta and sausage ragu. For dinner, order up the radicchio salad, one of Portland's best, along with spaghetti carbonara and grilled lamb chops.

3808 N Williams Ave

37 Toro Bravo

Toro Bravo is about as Spanish as the French revolution. (Have you ever seen a pulled pork sandwich in a restaurant in Madrid?) But regardless of nationality, John Gorham's flagship spot consistently serves damn good food, and stays true to the trifecta of Spanish cuisine: garlic, paprika, and religion. The rules haven't changed since Toro's debut in 2007: Get there right when it opens at five and be ready to fight for your spot.

120 NE Russell St
Portland, OR 97212

38 The Woodsman Tavern

Named one of GQ's best new restaurants of 2011, the perennially buzzing Woodsman Tavern borrows from myriad big-city influences Balthazar's seafood tower, Hog & Rocks' ham plates but brings it all down to earth. Surprisingly, lines have yet to form for weekend brunch, despite deeply comforting dishes like potted egg with greens.
