      Weekend Guide

      What to do when you've got 36 hours to get to know a city.

      • 36 Hours in Savannah, Ga. JUN 3

      • 36 Hours in Chapel Hill-Carrboro, N.C. MAY 27

      • 36 Hours in Bilbao, Spain MAY 20

      • 36 Hours in Medellín, Colombia MAY 13

      • 36 Hours in San Luis Obispo, Calif. MAY 6

      Less than an hour away from the concrete sprawl of Guatemala City, Antigua feels like a portal to another century. The nearly 500-year-old city is cradled by three volcanoes and filled with colonial-era mansions, churches and convents, many with orange and yellow facades. In the evening, residents gather in the jacaranda-lined plazas to listen to musicians tapping mallets on marimbas, or wander between lively night spots. Once the capital of this Central American country (before a series of devastating earthquakes forced the colonial government to move to Guatemala City in the late 1700s), Antigua, population 41,000, is a Unesco World Heritage site. Yet in addition to the striking 17th- and 18th-century ruins right in the center of town, the city offers an array of restaurants, boutiques and bars with live music. Visitors can browse the craft markets or sip an espresso at a working coffee farm, while those with a more adventurous streak can hike up one of the looming volcanoes, or strap on a helmet, lock in a carabiner and hurtle through the rain forest on a zip wire.

      Friday

      1. Guatemala’s Picasso | 4 p.m.

      In the hills above Antigua, past painted carousel horses and brick warrior statues placed along the roadside, lies the Santo Domingo del Cerro Cultural Park, a free outdoor sculpture gallery dedicated to the whimsical works of Efraín Recinos, the late artist and architect who is sometimes referred to as Guatemala’s Picasso. Mr. Recinos, who designed the country’s national theater in the capital in the shape of a crouching jaguar, was a prolific artist whose colorful murals adorn some of Guatemala’s most important buildings, including the National Music Conservatory, La Aurora International Airport and the National Library of Guatemala. In Santo Domingo del Cerro park, Mr. Recinos’s tongue-in-cheek installations are complemented by other artists’ works: cartoonish cutout figures perched atop a pavilion, a rusted-out VW bug that appears poised to fly, and tubular neon orange sculptures against a backdrop of volcanoes.

      2. Courtyard Dining | 7 p.m.

      Between La Merced Church and the Plaza Mayor in the center of town you’ll find Micho’s Gastro Pub, a two-year-old spot popular for both dinner and cocktails. The décor inside looks a bit like a college bar from the 1980s, so grab a table in the outdoor courtyard with a large shell-shaped fountain. The four-course set menu includes creative delights like jalapeño mac and cheese, a lamb tamale spiced with fenugreek and achiote, and an avocado crème brûlée. Expect to pay around 450 quetzales, or about $60, at 7.30 quetzales to the dollar, for two with cocktails.

      3. Mezcal and Music | 9 p.m.

      Entered through a refrigerator door, the back-room parlor at the music dive Café No Sé has a stuffed mountain goat head on the wall and serves its own brand of smooth, smoky mezcal. Popular enough to be sold in bars in the United States, the bar’s label, Ilegal Mezcal, is produced in Oaxaca, Mexico, and comes in three varieties; the añejo (old) batch is well worth ordering. Up front, in a candlelit, checkered-floor space with curtains made from coffee sacks, musicians perform most nights of the week. On a recent evening, Ishto Juevez, a Guatemalan singer-songwriter with shaggy hair and a neck scarf, strummed folk songs on an acoustic guitar and sang in Spanish.

      Saturday

      4. Ancient and Modern | 9 a.m.

      After grabbing a strong coffee and an espresso brownie at the Refuge Coffee Bar, a specialty roaster with brewing equipment resembling something out of Dr. Frankenstein’s laboratory, head to the fascinating Pre-Columbian and Glass Art Museum, one of five museums inside the half-ruined, half-renovated Casa Santo Domingo monastery complex. Here, millenniums-old Maya artifacts like jaguar masks and funerary urns are displayed alongside glass sculptures of a more recent vintage. The juxtapositions are meant to highlight the similarities in topic and form between pre-Columbian designs and art from later societies, such as crystal vases by Lalique. In one exhibit, a figurative Maya incense burner is contrasted with a Swedish sculptor’s glass mask. In nearly all cases, the ancient art outshines the modern pieces, but the juxtapositions are intriguing nonetheless.

      5. Lush Lunch | 12 p.m.

      In recent years, many restaurants have opened inside colonial-era houses, with tables set up in lush gardens or courtyards with fountains. Saberíco, an organic Guatemalan restaurant in a blooming garden with painted wood tables, ivy-accented stone walls and chirping blue parakeets, is an ideal spot for a leisurely lunch. Specialties include subanik, a spicy stew of chicken, pork and beef rib served with a tamale, and pepian, a chicken dish with roasted tomatoes, cinnamon, and pumpkin seeds, and scooped up with black corn tortillas. Lunch for two costs around 200 quetzales. Afterward, choose from one of 74 ice cream flavors at Sobremesa Helados Exóticos, a small shop offering creative, hand-churned options like lavender-blueberry and pistachio-cardamom.

      6. Zip Lines or Coffee? | 1:45 p.m.

      A bumpy Jeep ride up the mountains brings visitors to a densely forested spot overlooking scenic coffee plantations where they strap on harnesses, get clipped onto zip lines and race on wires between treetops or across a vast canyon. Antigua Canopy Tours offers two options, both exhilarating: The forest tour involves seven 130-foot zip lines though the rain forest, while the canyon tour has visitors riding a pair of 1,700-foot lines strung up across a deep ravine. Start in the treetops, then graduate to the towering heights of the canyon, hurtling hundreds of feet through the air, and enjoying the speed and spectacular views ($50 for one tour, $75 for both. Note: Some businesses in Antigua quote prices in dollars). Those with a preference for earthbound delights can tour a working coffee farm down below at the Finca Filadelfia plantation ($18).

      7. Woodcuts and Sandals | 4 p.m.

      Antigua is a shopper’s paradise. Situated next to the famous Santa Catalina Arch, Nim Po’t is a sprawling emporium of artisanal Guatemalan products, from beautiful traditional clothing (431 quetzales) to woodcuts inspired by the Maya calendar (350 quetzales) and woven iPad covers (80 quetzales). Colibri specializes in high-quality textiles created by a network of some 500 Maya women in 25 small villages. Gorgeous tablecloths start at $32, and wide, colorful scarves go for $24. Uxibal is the place to go for stylish, handmade shoes and bags, like a tasseled pair of suede sandals with a pretty yellow fabric design ($99) and a slouchy brown leather purse with an embroidered textile panel ($215).

      8. Tasting Menu | 7 p.m.

      For relaxed fine dining in the courtyard of a 120-year-old private house, head to Angeline, which focuses on local ingredients. On a visit last summer the menu featured Vietnamese summer rolls and dorado marinated in lemon tea. These days, Angeline serves an ambitious multi-course tasting menu with dishes like shrimp flavored with saffron and pink pepper cream, and brisket with orzo. There is a cocktail bar in a red-and-white space just off the courtyard. The seven-course tasting menu costs 500 quetzales per person.

      9. Rooftop Views | 9 p.m.

      Although it is only three stories up, the rooftop at Café Sky offers a great vantage point in this low-rise town. The bar, with its mosaic-topped tables and year-round Christmas lights, is a fun spot to admire the views while sipping a zesty michelada made with your pick of beers (try Gallo). Continue to the cozy wine shop Tabacos y Vinos where you can enjoy Argentine or Uruguayan wine at a communal table.

      Sunday

      10. Guatemalan Breakfast | 7 a.m.

      The streets are quiet on Sunday morning when you make your way to El Portón, a friendly diner with red-and-yellow tiled floors and an employee frying masa tortillas on a griddle near the entrance. Have a hearty breakfast of eggs, black beans, fried plantains, fresh cheese and coffee at a picnic-style table to prepare yourself for a volcano hike. If you have time before the shuttle leaves, explore the handicrafts market at nearby Parque Central, Antigua’s principal plaza.

      11. Volcanoes, Up Close | 8 a.m.

      You’ve been looking up at them, now climb one. The volcanoes that surround Antigua give the town much of its character, but to appreciate them requires getting close. Horses are always an option on the active Pacaya volcano, as locals follow you up the steep hillside, tempting you with the chance to ride rather than climb the 8,373-foot peak. But the orange glow of lava rewards the long hike, as do the marshmallows that the guides bring out of their packs to roast in the geothermal heat. The views of Lake Amatitlán below are wonderful, even shrouded in fog. The more challenging climb, however, is the twin-peaked Acatenango volcano, which at over 13,000 feet goes through four distinct ecosystems, and overlooks a simmering sister volcano called Fuego, which spews lava on a regular basis. The trip to Pacaya takes seven hours, all told, and the Acatenango excursion takes 12. Many travel agencies in Antigua can arrange guided treks up the volcanoes, starting around 100 quetzales.

      Lodging

      The Hotel Casa Santo Domingo offers 128 antiques-filled rooms inside a partly restored 16th-century monastery complex. There are five museums on the grounds, including the Pre-Columbian and Glass Art Museum, and the Museum of Colonial Arts. (Third Calle Oriente No. 28; casasantodomingo.com.gt; doubles from $160).

      Across the street from the ruins of the Capuchinas convent, El Convento Boutique Hotel has 26 stylish rooms, an outdoor pool, free Wi-fi and a restaurant with excellent contemporary Guatemalan fare. (Second Avenida Norte No. 11; elconventoantigua.com; doubles from $139).

      Correction: April 9, 2015

      An earlier version of a map with this article gave an incorrect street address for Micho’s Gastro Pub, a restaurant and bar in Antigua, Guatemala. It is 4 Calle Oriente No. 10, not Fourth Avenida Norte No. 10.

      Correction: April 19, 2015
      The 36 Hours column last Sunday, about Antigua, Guatemala, misstated the street address for Micho’s Gastro Pub, a restaurant and bar. It is 4 Calle Oriente No.10, not Fourth Avenida Norte No.10.
