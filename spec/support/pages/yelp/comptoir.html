        3/23/2015

        For those who don't know, Le Comptoir has become one of the most wildly popular and hyped restaurants in all of Paris. Some of the write-ups about this place warn that reservations for dinner have to be made 4-7 months in advance. Fortunately for lunch you can just walk in, which is what we did, but you have to get there just before noon when it opens, as getting there any later will require you to wait outside in a growing line. This place has been hyped to death and its chef, Yves Camdeborde, has become one of the most highly revered (or feared) in the whole city. Le Comptoir is the kind of place that's been elevated so much by all the high praise that cynics like me have to wonder whether it will actually live up to the hype.

        For starters, my wife ordered a small croques monsieur with salmon and caviar, while I got the escargots (I wanted something tasty but light) and our friend got what was basically raw, sashimi-like salmon with a kind of cream and clumps of pickled onion. All three were great, but I thought my wife's was the best, hands down, as it offered a beautifully rich flavor. The way the salmon melded with the melted cheese was absolutely heavenly.

        For the main dishes, my wife ordered one of the specials of the day, which was basically extremely tender chunks of stewed veal in a kind of mustard-based sauce which came with a small scoop of mustard herb ice cream on top. The waiter told her this was a special dish that doesn't make it on the menu often. It had an incredibly unique flavor - and the taste of the veal was to die for. This was bursting with flavor and was well seasoned too. The mustard sauce almost threatened to overpower the meat, but once you started chewing, the tender meat's rich, earthy flavor came through in spades. The mustard ice cream melted and fused with the sauce, adding a kind of creamy element. This was a creative, flavorful, and well conceived dish.

        I opted for the braised lamb shank, which came in a sauce with a side of couscous with these massive, plump, juicy prunes and apricots. I wasn't wild about these sides at all (not because they were bad, I'm just not wild about cous cous and prunes), but the lamb shank was cooked to perfection. It did, however, need just a tiny pinch of salt, but once that was taken care of, we were in business, and this was probably one of the best (juiciest, most tender and flavorful) braised lamb shanks I've ever had in a restaurant. I even dug the bit of marrow out of the end of the bone for that little dab of intense lamb-y flavor.

        Our friend ordered a lamb chop that came in a sauce with white beans and bell peppers. He was coming down with a sore throat, so we didn't sample each other's dishes, but he seemed pretty content with it.

        To drink we shared a mini-bottle of house red that suited the dishes perfectly.

        Overall it was an incredibly fulfilling meal. We would come back. But did it live up to the hype? Well, I can't say that these were the most mind-blowing dishes I've ever had, but they were extremely good. So, yes, it was absolutely wonderful, and it was a fun experience overall. Le Comptoir is probably best judged for its impossible-to-get-a-table dinner services, where diners eat solely from a special €50 prix fixe menu devised by the chef each evening. I suspect those may exhibit a little more personal flair and creativity from the chef. But we're not the kind of people who are willing to eat at a place where a table has to be reserved half a year in advance, so we may never experience that.

        On the carrefour de L'Odeon, just a few steps from boulevard Saint Germain, stands a restaurant that I have been wanting to eat at for a long time. Ever since Anthony Bourdain extolled its virtues during his Paris episode, Le Comptoir du Relais has been on my bucket list of must-try foodie destinations. Bourdain rarely gets it wrong, and I have to say that the food here did not disappoint.

        But just getting in is half the battle. If you don't have a reservation, the time to come is during lunch. Guests of the accompanying hotel are given priority during dinner, so reservations can be really hard to com by. But if you just want to taste what this restaurant offers, they take in the lunch crowd on a first come, first served basis; a line starts to form about 15 minutes before they open at 11:30.

        I highly recommend the Toast de Foie Gras. It was an object lesson in balance and contrast. The rich slices of foie were perfectly counterbalanced by the salt, pickled onions, and fruit jam. And the toast and accompanying salad provided a nice textural contrast to the creamy and unctuous liver. A great but decadent way to begin the meal.

        The Tartare de Beouf was delicious and unique. I really loved the thought put into this dish. The finely chopped raw beef was made even more rich with the gooey egg, but it was offset by the textural crunch of the croutons and the salsa verde which added some nice acid and flavor. But the use of purple shiso really made the dish. It lifted the beef tartare with its bright, herbal, almost Asian-inspired flavor. The only downside of the dish was that the croutons tasted of rancid oil. But the dish as a whole was really a standout.

        My main complaint, and perhaps the reason I am hesitant to give this place 5 stars, is because the woman who took my order -- and runs the place, it appears -- seemed rather impatient and put out that her restaurant was such a popular place. Texting my order into an iPhone, she never made eye contact while I was ordering, never smiled, and never said thank you. This impertinence was not just for me either; she was this way with the French couple sitting next to me too.

        But that aside, this is really a terrific restaurant with outstanding and creative food. You might want to brush up on your French before coming here though, or use a translator app on your phone, because there is no English menu, and the staff is too harried to translate for you. For when the poor tourist sitting next to me ordered poulpe rôti (roast octopus) thinking it was poulet rôti (roast chicken), she was quite shocked when her dish arrived. She tried returning it (twice!), until she just went ahead and ate it. Turns out she liked it after all. Which goes to show that even when she didn't want it, she still enjoyed it. Yes, the food is that good.

        4 1/2 stars

        Have you ever waited in line for something just because everyone else was waiting in line? If being at an "it" place is priority, then step right up. If you're invested in the actual pleasure of the experience... keep walking.

        While service and quality at Le Comptoir has been steadily declining over the last year, the wait time for unimpressive food and brusque service has not!

        The Croque Monsieur with caviar and smoked salmon, or the Sandwich de Ocean Sodium, is almost uncomfortable to eat. Did you want some water to try and wash down the copious amounts of salty brine burning your throat? That'll be 6 euros, sucker. (Tourist pro-tip: charging anywhere from 3-9 euros for water isn't uncommon, and they're not going to tell you, so BYOW if you're on a budget.)

        The free bread we had prior to ordering was better than the bread our sandwiches were served on, so points for the free bread. The escargot was also fantastic, but not unique in any way.

        Oh, and Le Comptoir's little to-go restaurant next door makes waffles with Nutella that are soggy and taste like county fair... so that's pretty good... if you're drunk. Maybe.

        Now, I know it can be jarring trying to figure out where to eat in Paris, but listening to something Anthony Bourdain said years ago isn't reason enough to waste the stomach space.
